# **`THE INTERNET IS BROKEN. NYC CAN FIX IT.`**

New York City has an access problem. Applying for a job. Helping your kid with homework. Scheduling a doctor’s appointment. For millions of New Yorkers, these seemingly straightforward tasks become impossible \- or impossibly expensive thanks to the lack of broadband access.

Internet access in this city tracks race and class with brutal precision. [Nearly](https://nycfuture.org/research/understanding-and-overcoming-the-bronx-digital-divide) 1 in 4 Bronx households lack broadband, almost double Manhattan’s rate. When federal subsidies ended in 2024, 44% of Bronx households lost their connection overnight. 

ISPs build where it’s profitable, not where it’s needed. They lock in landlords, lock out competition, and charge New Yorkers [up to $299/month](https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/outreach-and-education/household-financial-stability/assessing-cost-of-broadband-for-low-and-moderate-income-communities) for mediocre speeds. One-third of Black and Hispanic households have no broadband at all. This isn’t a market failure. It’s a policy choice  and NYC can make a different one.

This isn’t just about streaming or scrolling. It’s about working class New Yorkers accessing their basic necessities like applying for jobs, filling out college applications, attending classes, scheduling doctor’s appointments, and applying for benefits. Digital exclusion deepens existing inequality.

## **Private ISPs have failed New York over and over**

Verizon, Spectrum, and Optimum [don’t compete](https://www.cnet.com/home/internet/my-internet-provider-is-a-monopoly-heres-why-yours-probably-is-too/). They carve up territory and charge whatever they want. The City [sued](https://www.nytimes.com/2017/03/13/nyregion/ny-sues-verizon-fios.html) Verizon for skipping underserved neighborhoods. The [state](https://www.theverge.com/2018/12/18/18146210/charter-spectrum-174-million-settlement-new-york-state-attorney-general-internet-speeds) fined Spectrum $174 million for lying about speeds. Altice was [caught](https://www.nydailynews.com/new-york/ny-metro-altice-agreement-overcharge-20190811-6tt2g57sx5gixd5isfayqwwhbi-story.html) overcharging customers. They cut exclusive deals with landlords, block competing providers, and offer “customer service” that’s become a citywide punchline.

The [2020 Internet Master Plan](https://www.nyc.gov/assets/cto/downloads/internet-master-plan/NYC_IMP_1.7.20_FINAL-2.pdf) was supposed to change this. The City had allocated $157 million, vetted contractors, and was in the early stages of deployment. Then the Adams administration killed it and replaced it with Big Apple Connect, a program that hands [$38 million](https://citymeetings.nyc/meetings/new-york-city-council/2025-04-29-1000-am-committee-on-technology/chapter/cost-and-funding-details-of-the-big-apple-connect-program/) a year to the very same ISPs that created this crisis. Worse, Big Apple Connect [secretly](https://nysfocus.com/2025/08/25/nypd-surveillance-nycha-big-apple-connect) connected NYCHA security cameras to NYPD’s surveillance system, without telling residents, NYCHA, or City Council. 

That's what happens when the City treats the internet as a corporate subsidy instead of public infrastructure.


## **The city already has the infrastructure.**

There’s fiber running to 1,800+ schools, hundreds of libraries, and NYCHA buildings across all five boroughs. CityNet connects hundreds of city agencies. The City controls the underground conduit and can require new fiber whenever streets are opened. The infrastructure exists. It’s just not being used for you. Public ownership would change that.

Municipal broadband [works](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1YfCpLdDUjbGmbhspR9H0FUdY-Y7Pr-89nVAJllUWHmc/edit?usp=sharing). When cities own the pipes and let providers compete on equal terms, prices drop and service improves. That’s open-access: city-owned fiber, real competition, no more monopoly pricing. It’s working in [towns](https://www.governor.ny.gov/news/governor-hochul-majority-leader-schumer-and-senator-gillibrand-award-over-140-million) across New York State and the [country](https://communitynets.org/content/community-network-map). Jamestown, NY offers gigabit service for $55 a month. Chattanooga, TN built a public network and received $2.69 billion in economic returns, 9,500 jobs, and a revitalized downtown. Internet for All is fighting to make it work in NYC, the world’s wealthiest city. 

# **Internet for New Yorkers, built by New Yorkers.**

We can’t wait for ISPs to do the right thing. The City needs to act now, and for the long term. 

There are two steps New York can take right now to fix this: get people online immediately, and build the foundation for a system that works for everyone.

### 1\. Get people online now. 

Thousands of New Yorkers lost internet access overnight in 2025, when federal subsidies ended. The City needs to act like it’s an emergency. Distribute hotspots to disconnected households. Expand device lending through libraries. Use schools as distribution points. Scale community networks like NYC Mesh that already provide free or affordable service in neighborhoods ISPs have abandoned. Pair connectivity with digital literacy programs through libraries and community centers. Create union apprentice pathways so the people building this infrastructure come from the communities it serves. Revive partnerships the Adams administration abandoned, including with [worker-owned cooperatives](https://broadbandbreakfast.com/nyc-worker-owned-isp-faces-uncertain-future-amid-shifting-city-broadband-policy/) founded by union members who were already delivering broadband to public housing before Big Apple Connect [ripped out their equipment](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2023/02/wtf-new-york-city-remove-free-broadband-favor-charter). At the same time, enforce the rules already on the books. ISPs are legally required to serve all neighborhoods. Landlords are legally required to let providers in. The City just hasn’t made them. 

2\. Build the foundation for public ownership.   
Establish a municipal broadband authority with real power \- not just to study the problem, but to own and operate infrastructure citywide, and to enforce the franchise agreements ISPs have violated for decades. Adopt “dig-once” policies: whenever the city opens a street for water, gas, or subways, lay fiber at the same time if it’s in an underserved neighborhood. This costs a fraction of standalone construction.Connect the fiber that already runs to schools, libraries, hospitals, and NYCHA. Start in the neighborhoods that have been left behind. 

All major deployments should prioritize union labor through CWA and IBEW at prevailing wages, with defined benefit pensions, and apprenticeship pathways so workers from neighborhoods getting connected are the ones doing the building.
